Core Legal Arguments

The case rests on key legal principles under Tennessee law. These concepts show why Progressive's position is untenable and why their duty to pay the claim is absolute.

Possession vs. Ownership

In law, having physical control (possession) of an item is different from having legal title (ownership). This distinction is crucial.

In This Case:

Progressive took **possession** of the vehicle on May 19, 2025, moving it to their salvage yard. While the paper **title** was still in the insured's name, Progressive had functional control. Tennessee's salvage law is triggered by this transfer of control, not just the paper title.

The Prevention Doctrine

A fundamental rule of contract law: You cannot prevent someone from fulfilling a condition and then blame them for not fulfilling it.

In This Case:

Progressive demanded a bonded title, a process that requires possession of the vehicle. By taking the vehicle, Progressive made it **impossible** for the insured to comply. Therefore, the condition is legally excused, and Progressive must pay.

Insurer's Statutory Duty

Tennessee Code § 55-3-212(b) places a specific duty on insurance companies in total loss situations.

In This Case:

The law requires the insurer to apply for a salvage title once it "acquires ownership" by paying a total loss claim and taking the vehicle. By taking possession with the intent to salvage, Progressive triggered its own legal duty to handle the title paperwork.

Impossible Condition

A party cannot enforce a contract condition that is impossible to perform, especially when they caused the impossibility.

In This Case:

The demand for a bonded title is an impossible condition. The bonded title process (T.C.A. § 55-3-103) is for people who *have* a car but no title. The insured does not have the car. Progressive's demand is a legal absurdity designed to delay payment.

Unfair Claims Practices & Bad Faith

Progressive's conduct appears to violate multiple provisions of the Tennessee Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act (T.C.A. § 56-8-105). These violations are strong evidence of bad faith, which can lead to a 25% penalty under T.C.A. § 56-7-105.
